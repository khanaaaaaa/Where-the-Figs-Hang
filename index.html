1932 — 1963

Sylvia Plath

"I took a deep breath and listened to the old brag of my heart: I am, I am, I am."

1932

Born on October 27 in Boston, Massachusetts, to Otto Plath and Aurelia Schober.

1940

Her father dies of complications from diabetes. She is eight years old. The loss shapes everything that follows.

1950

Enrolls at Smith College on scholarship. Begins publishing poetry and short stories in national magazines.

1953

Guest editor at Mademoiselle in New York. Returns home and attempts suicide. Undergoes electroconvulsive therapy.

1955

Graduates summa cum laude from Smith. Wins a Fulbright scholarship to Newnham College, Cambridge.

1956

Meets and marries poet Ted Hughes in Cambridge.

1960

Publishes her first poetry collection, The Colossus. Her daughter Frieda is born.

1962

Separates from Ted Hughes after discovering his affair. Writes the Ariel poems in a white heat — one a day, before dawn.

1963

Publishes The Bell Jar under the pseudonym Victoria Lucas. Dies on February 11, aged 30.

The Bell Jar 1963

Her only novel. A semi-autobiographical account of a young woman's descent into mental illness. Raw, darkly comic, and devastatingly honest.

Ariel 1965

Published posthumously. Considered one of the most powerful poetry collections of the 20th century. Confessional, fierce, and luminous.

The Colossus 1960

Her debut collection. More restrained than Ariel, but already showing the precision and darkness that would define her voice.

The Journals of Sylvia Plath 1982

Unfiltered entries spanning her life. A window into her ambition, her grief, and her relentless inner world.

Letters Home 1975

Correspondence with her mother. Reveals the gap between her public brightness and private suffering.

Sylvia Plath was awarded the Pulitzer Prize for Poetry posthumously in 1982 for her Collected Poems — one of only a handful of poets to receive it after death.

She is credited with pioneering confessional poetry alongside Robert Lowell and Anne Sexton — a movement that made the interior life a legitimate subject of literature.

The Bell Jar has never gone out of print. It remains one of the most widely read novels about mental illness, identity, and the pressures placed on women.

Her grave in Heptonstall, Yorkshire, is a site of quiet pilgrimage. Visitors leave pens — an offering to the writer who never stopped.

"Dying is an art, like everything else. I do it exceptionally well."
