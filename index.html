Lady Lazarus
Sylvia Plath, 1962

I have done it again.

One year in every ten

I manage it --


A sort of walking miracle, my skin

Bright as a Nazi lampshade,

My right foot


A paperweight,

My face a featureless, fine

Jew linen.


Peel off the napkin

O my enemy.

Do I terrify?


The nose, the eye pits, the full set of teeth?

The sour breath

Will vanish in a day.


Soon, soon the flesh

The grave cave ate will be

At home on me


And I a smiling woman.

I am only thirty.

And like the cat I have nine times to die.


This is Number Three.

What a trash

To annihilate each decade.


What a million filaments.

The peanut-crunching crowd

Shoves in to see


Them unwrap me hand and foot --

The big strip tease.

Gentlemen, ladies


These are my hands

My knees.

I may be skin and bone,


Nevertheless, I am the same, identical woman.

The first time it happened I was ten.

It was an accident.


The second time I meant

To last it out and not come back at all.

I rocked shut


As a seashell.

They had to call and call

And pick the worms off me like sticky pearls.


Dying

Is an art, like everything else.

I do it exceptionally well.


I do it so it feels like hell.

I do it so it feels real.

I guess you could say I've a call.


It's easy enough to do it in a cell.

It's easy enough to do it and stay put.

It's the theatrical


Comeback in broad day

To the same place, the same face, the same brute

Amused shout:


'A miracle!'

That knocks me out.

There is a charge


For the eyeing of my scars, there is a charge

For the hearing of my heart --

It really goes.


And there is a charge, a very large charge

For a word or a touch

Or a bit of blood


Or a piece of my hair or my clothes.

So, so, Herr Doktor.

So, Herr Enemy.


I am your opus,

I am your valuable,

The pure gold baby


That melts to a shriek.

I turn and burn.

Do not think I underestimate your great concern.


Ash, ash --

You poke and stir.

Flesh, bone, there is nothing there --


A cake of soap,

A wedding ring,

A gold filling.


Herr God, Herr Lucifer

Beware

Beware.


Out of the ash

I rise with my red hair

And I eat men like air.

I. Life
1932-1963

Life

1932

Born October 27 in Boston, Massachusetts, to Otto Plath, a German-born entomologist, and Aurelia Schober. She is the eldest of two children.

1940

Her father Otto dies of complications from diabetes -- an illness he refused to treat. Sylvia is eight. The loss becomes the wound she writes from for the rest of her life.

1950

Enrolls at Smith College on a full scholarship. Begins publishing in Seventeen magazine and The Christian Science Monitor. Writes obsessively in her journals.

1952

Wins Mademoiselle magazine's fiction contest. Her story "Sunday at the Mintons'" is published. She is twenty and already a nationally recognised writer.

1953

Guest editor at Mademoiselle in New York. Attempts suicide by taking sleeping pills. Found three days later. Undergoes electroconvulsive therapy at McLean Hospital.

1955

Graduates summa cum laude from Smith. Wins a Fulbright Scholarship to Newnham College, Cambridge.

1956

Meets Ted Hughes at a Cambridge party on February 25. She bites his cheek. They marry four months later, on June 16.

1960

Publishes her debut poetry collection The Colossus. Daughter Frieda Rebecca is born on April 1. The family settles in London.

1962

Discovers Hughes is having an affair. They separate. She moves to London with the children, to a flat on the same street where W.B. Yeats once lived. Writes the Ariel poems -- one a day, before the children wake.

1963

Publishes The Bell Jar under the pseudonym Victoria Lucas in January. On February 11, she dies by gas. She is thirty years old.

II. Work
The Collected

Work

The Bell Jar 1963

Her only novel, published under the pseudonym Victoria Lucas one month before her death. A semi-autobiographical account of Esther Greenwood's mental breakdown. Raw, darkly comic, devastatingly honest. Never out of print.

Ariel 1965

Published posthumously. The collection she wrote in the final months of her life -- one poem a day, before dawn. Lady Lazarus, Daddy, and Edge redefined what poetry could say. One of the most important collections of the 20th century.

The Colossus 1960

Her debut collection. More restrained than Ariel but already showing the precision and darkness that would define her. The title poem addresses her dead father as a ruined colossus she can never reconstruct.

The Journals 1982

Unfiltered and extraordinary. Covers 1950 to 1962. A window into her ambition, grief, and relentless self-examination. Two journals from her final years were destroyed by Ted Hughes.

Letters Home 1975

Correspondence with her mother Aurelia. The gap between the brightness of the letters and what we know of her inner life is one of the most haunting things in all of literature.

III. Legacy
Pulitzer 1982

Legacy

Pulitzer Prize, 1982. Awarded posthumously for her Collected Poems. She is one of very few poets to receive the Pulitzer after death.

Confessional poetry. Alongside Robert Lowell and Anne Sexton, Plath made the interior life, the body, and mental illness valid subjects for serious literature.

The Bell Jar has never gone out of print since 1963. Translated into dozens of languages. Assigned in schools and universities worldwide.

The grave at Heptonstall. Her grave in Yorkshire is a site of quiet pilgrimage. Visitors leave pens. The name on the stone reads Sylvia Plath Hughes. For decades, people scratched out the name Hughes. It was re-engraved repeatedly.

The Ariel controversy. The Ariel Hughes published in 1965 was not the collection Plath had arranged. Her original manuscript was published in 2004 as Ariel: The Restored Edition.
