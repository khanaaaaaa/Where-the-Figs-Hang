Sylvia Plath

Life

Life

1932

Born October 27 in Boston, Massachusetts.

1940

Her father dies. She is eight. The loss shapes everything.

1950

Enrolls at Smith College on scholarship. Begins publishing nationally.

1953

Guest editor at Mademoiselle. Attempts suicide. Undergoes electroconvulsive therapy.

1955

Graduates summa cum laude. Wins Fulbright to Cambridge.

1956

Meets and marries Ted Hughes.

1960

Publishes The Colossus. Daughter Frieda is born.

1962

Separates from Hughes. Writes the Ariel poems — one a day, before dawn.

1963

Publishes The Bell Jar. Dies February 11, aged 30.

Work

Work

The Bell Jar 1963

Her only novel. Semi-autobiographical, raw, darkly comic, and devastatingly honest about mental illness and identity.

Ariel 1965

Published posthumously. One of the most powerful poetry collections of the 20th century — confessional, fierce, luminous.

The Colossus 1960

Her debut collection. Restrained but already showing the precision and darkness that would define her voice.

The Journals 1982

Unfiltered entries spanning her life. A window into her ambition, grief, and relentless inner world.

Letters Home 1975

Correspondence with her mother — revealing the gap between her public brightness and private suffering.

Legacy

Legacy

Awarded the Pulitzer Prize for Poetry posthumously in 1982 for her Collected Poems.

Pioneered confessional poetry alongside Robert Lowell and Anne Sexton — making the interior life a legitimate subject of literature.

The Bell Jar has never gone out of print. It remains one of the most widely read novels about mental illness and the pressures placed on women.

Her grave in Heptonstall, Yorkshire, is a site of quiet pilgrimage. Visitors leave pens — an offering to the writer who never stopped.
